Asbestos

Plain-language reference page for asbestos in safety and industrial hygiene contexts.

Last updated: 2026-01-01

Back to Topics Index

Description

Asbestos is a group of naturally occurring fibrous minerals used in building materials and industrial products. It appears in safety and industrial hygiene contexts because legacy materials may release fibers during disturbance.

Quick Facts

  • Hazard type: airborne fiber exposure.
  • Common contexts: construction, maintenance, renovation, demolition, legacy equipment.
  • Primary regulators: OSHA, EPA, and related federal programs.
  • Sampling methods: air and bulk material evaluation referenced by official methods.
  • Disposal regulators: EPA and applicable state environmental agencies.
  • Exposure pathway: inhalation.
  • OELs: PEL, REL, TLV.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is asbestos?

Asbestos is a set of fibrous minerals referenced by OSHA and EPA materials for occupational and environmental contexts. Detailed definitions appear in official program pages and standards listings.

Where is this commonly encountered?

Asbestos is commonly associated with legacy building materials and industrial products identified in OSHA, EPA, and NIOSH guidance. Specific examples are documented in those sources.

Why is this a concern in occupational settings?

Occupational concern centers on airborne fiber exposure addressed in OSHA and NIOSH source materials. The basis is described in those sources.

Who regulates this hazard in the workplace?

OSHA provides workplace regulatory requirements for asbestos-related activities. The applicable standards are listed in OSHA regulations.

Are there environmental or non-workplace regulations that apply?

EPA programs address asbestos in environmental and public health contexts. Applicable programs are listed by EPA.

Are there applicable consensus standards or guidance documents?

Consensus standards and guidance are published by organizations such as ASTM, ANSI, and ACGIH. Specific documents are listed by those organizations.

How is exposure to this hazard typically evaluated?

Evaluation references official sampling and analytical methods described by OSHA, EPA, and NIOSH. The method listings identify applicable approaches.

What analytical or test methods are commonly referenced?

Common references include OSHA analytical methods and NIOSH NMAM methods, with additional ASTM standards. These sources identify method names and numbers.

Are exposure limits or guidelines established for this hazard?

Exposure limits and guidelines are established in regulatory or consensus sources such as OSHA and ACGIH. The specific values are provided in those sources.

What health effects are associated with exposure?

Health effects are summarized in OSHA, NIOSH, and EPA materials. Details are documented in those sources.

Are effects acute, chronic, or both?

Source materials describe the temporal nature of asbestos-related health effects. Specific characterizations appear in those sources.

Is latency a consideration for this hazard?

Latency is addressed in OSHA, NIOSH, and EPA informational materials. The associated context is provided by those sources.

What types of controls are commonly referenced for this hazard?

Controls are described in OSHA standards and related guidance sources. Detailed requirements are in the referenced documents.

Are training or program requirements commonly associated with this hazard?

Training and program requirements are addressed in OSHA standards and related guidance. Specific requirements are provided by the source documents.

How is this hazard addressed during maintenance, renovation, or demolition?

Maintenance, renovation, and demolition activities are covered under OSHA and EPA programs. Relevant requirements are listed in those programs.

How is waste containing this material typically regulated or classified?

Waste handling and classification references are addressed in EPA programs. Specific classifications are defined in those sources.

Are there transportation or disposal considerations?

Transportation and disposal considerations are addressed by EPA and other applicable agencies. Official requirements are listed in those sources.

Where can official guidance and regulatory text be found?

Official guidance and regulatory text are available from OSHA, EPA, and NIOSH program pages and standards listings. Use those sources for authoritative details.
